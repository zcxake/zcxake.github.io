（未完）Compositionally Graded KNN-Based Multilayer Composite with Excellent Piezoelectric Temperature Stability

创建 2022-05-21 更新 2022-06-03 分类 科研相关 本文字数： 19k 阅读时长约 ≈ 18 分

原文：http://crossmark.crossref.org/dialog/?doi=10.1002%2Fadma.202109175&domain=pdf&date_stamp=2022-01-18

摘要：无铅钾纳铌酸盐基陶瓷（lead-free potassium sodium niobate (KNN)- based ceramics）固有缺点就是压电系数$d_{33}$随温度变化不稳定，而这是由多晶相边界导致的。为此，本文通过设计具有多个连续相变的成分多层复合材料，提出一种结构梯度的概念。结构梯度陶瓷展现出优异的温度稳定性（其$d_{33}$在$25$-$100°C$温度范围内几乎没有变化，该品质远超于其他已报道的钾纳铌酸盐，这些陶瓷在同样的温度变化下压电系数变化超27%。连续相变、应变梯度、以及每层组分间的互补效应，三者协同作用使得压电系数表现出温度稳定性，且已被相场模拟的证明。这些发现有望为设计高稳定稳定性的功能材料提供范例。

引言

具有良好机电性能的压电材料在各种电子设备中起着重要作用，如传感器、传动器等。鉴于可持续发展的需求，无铅压电材料得到了广泛关注，其中，钾纳铌酸盐基的钙钛矿铁电陶瓷因其可能具有高压电性和高居里温度最具发展前景，自21世纪以来被大量研究。对于压电传感应用，材料品质因数是其压电系数$d_{33}$与工作温度范围。只是压电系数高是不够的，还需要其在$-20$-$100°C$温度范围内保持温度，这样才能在大跨度的工作温度下，保持可靠与稳定的传感输出，从而改善灵敏度。然而，即使是最先进的钾纳铌酸盐基陶瓷也由于其$d_{33}$的温度不稳定性而在实际应用中受到限制（见图1a）。KNN温度稳定性较差的本质原因是：多态相边界（多态相边界，PPB：polymorphic phase boundary，对组分和温度敏感）的存在，这也是替代有铅压电材料的主要问题之一。很显然，虽然挑战很大，但我们需要想办法显著提高钾纳铌酸盐陶瓷的温度稳定性，来满足期间对可靠性的要求。

近几年，陆续有关于调节压电材料各种性能随温度变化关系的办法提出。大部分研究将重点放在了电学应变性质的温度稳定性上（电学应变性质：大信号压电应变系数，$d_{33}^*=S_{max}/E_{max}$，可以由$d_{33}^*-T$估算），很少有关注压电电荷系数（压电电荷系数：最直接的压电效应，可由$d_{33}-T$估算），尤其是关注$d_{33}$极差的温度稳定性的。因此，迫切需要探索一种打破当前困境的新方法。

对KNN基陶瓷，人们常通过构建相边界来提高$d_{33}$，虽然这是最有效的办法，但是同时也带来了和PPB（多态相边界）相关的温度极度不稳定性。我们设想，令多个相变温度覆盖工作温度范围，便能同时获得高$d_{33}$与良好的温度稳定性。为实现这一设想，本次研究中，我们设计了成分梯度的多层复合材料，以构建KNN陶瓷的连续向转变（见图 1c,d）。我们选择通过改变化学成分合成的有不同相变温度的陶瓷系统
$(1-x)(K_{0.48}Na_{0.52})(Nb_{0.955}Sb_{0.045})O_3-xBi_{0.5}Na_{0.5}ZrO_3-0.2\%Fe_2O_3(x=0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04)[(1−x)KNNS-xBNZ-Fe]$（见图 1c），利用tape casting法制造出成分梯度的多层材料（见图d）。本研究选用KNN是因为：KNN具有明显的压电性，且O-T相变温度高于$150°C$，并可通过调整BNZ的含量来控制O-T相变，通过添加$Fe_2O_3$作为助烧结剂改善烧结行为[27]。由于存在多个连续相变，相互扩散引起的应变梯度以及每个组分层间的互补效应，可以实现$d_{33}$的极高温度稳定性（在$25$-$100°C$温度范围内变化小于6%）（见图1d）。本工作提供了一种开发实用先进材料的设计方法。

但是为什么呢。

成果

抛光与热蚀刻陶瓷的横截面扫描电子显微镜图如图2，可以看到与成分相关的微观结构，相邻组分的边界也可由此和Bi与Zr元素的线扫描（图S1，支撑信息）进行区分。该微观结构表明我们获得了紧密连接的多层复合材料，可由图S2d的$\epsilon_r–T$图像看到顺电铁电相变$T_C$。图2a,b展示了分别用放大SEM和压电力显微镜（PFM）获得了复合材料每层组分的细节微观结构，每个单独组分的细节图在职称信息中的图S3。由tape casting法制得的陶瓷晶粒尺寸更加均匀精确，约在3-5$\mu m$量级，可以观察到每个组分中畴形状的多样性。较大尺寸的铁电畴出现在I和II层中，而在III和IV层中观察到了更复杂无规的铁电畴。整体而言，畴的尺寸随着BNZ（x）含量的增加而减小，对应于从正交相到多相共存的相变（见图S2，支撑信息）。相结构与畴尺寸间也有类似的现象，其中单正交相一般伴随较大尺寸的畴，而多相共存的陶瓷则由于共存相的能量相似性而具有大量小尺寸的畴。

采用微区X射线衍射(micro-XRD)进一步验证结构梯度信息。随着检测位置沿着横截面不同层间移动（如图2c所示），晶体结构明显随着BNZ含量的提升而改变，这一点由峰值强度和位置的变化所证实。

It is observed that the two {200}PC diffraction peaks gradually shift to a lower angle from composition I to composition IV due to the substitut ion of larger ionic radius of Zr4+ (0.072 nm) for Nb5+ (0.064 nm), accompanied by a change in peak intensity. Combined with the εr–T curves in Figure S2 in the Supporting Information, layers with compositions I and II have an orthorhombic (O) phase, as evidenced by the characteristic XRD peak shape and the far above room temperature TO-T.

While for layers with compositions III and IV, the shape vari-
ation of the two diffraction peaks and the gradually decreased
and diffused O-T phase transition demonstrate the coexistence
of O and T phases. Of particular importance is that the XRD
patterns detected at the interfaces (II–III and III–IV interfaces,
marked by stars in Figure 2c) are totally different from that
of the layer III, revealing the element interdiffusion between
layers.[29] Therefore, the SEM, PFM, and micro-XRD reveal
that the four constituent layers with different compositions
and crystal structures have been successfully integrated into a
hierarchically structured KNN ceramic with clear interdiffusion
feature between layers. However, the limited detection points of
micro-XRD still cannot reveal the entire structure evolution of
the multilayer composite, thus line scan Raman spectroscopy
is used to better understand the local structure information
and the formation of structural gradient in the studied KNN
multilayer composite (Figure 2d,e). The 3D colormap surface
with projection in Figure 2d shows the variation in Raman
spectra. The difference of the scattered signal roughly reflects
the composition zone and the interfaces, which is marked
by the white dotted lines. Group theory predicts that the fer-
roelectric orthorhombic phase of KNN perovskite structure has Amm2 space group, the Raman-active optical modes can
be expressed as 4A1+4B1+3B2+A2. The entire vibrational con-
figuration can be divided into two regions:[9,30–32] 1) low wave-
number region (≤200 cm−1), where the translational modes are
associated with the motion of alkaline cations (K+, Na+); 2) in
the range of 200 to 900 cm−1, there exist six vibrations associ-
ated with BO6 octahedra. Among which, υ1 (≈600 cm−1) and υ2
(≈550 cm−1) characteristic phonon modes represent the double
degenerate symmetric ONbO stretching vibration, revealing
the important details of lattice structure of BO6 octahedra.[30–32]
Consequently, the selected and magnified Raman spectra with
normalized intensity in the range from 400 to 800 cm−1 are dis-
played in Figure 2e, the Raman shifting and full-width at half-
maxima (FWHM) values of the two modes were extracted using
Lorentz fitting. As guided by the black dotted line in Figure 2e,
the composition variation from I to IV induces the changes in
both Raman shift and FWHM. On the one hand, the frequen-
cies of both υ1 and υ2 modes shift to lower values with the
increase of measurement distance (from I to IV), indicating the
decreased binding strength between B-site cations and oxygen
after introducing Sb, Bi, and Zr ions into KNN matrix.[12,33,34]
On the other hand, both the υ1 and υ2 modes become broad-
ened with the increase of measurement distance, especially
for the υ2 mode. The broadening of phonon vibrational mode is closely related to the anharmonic effect.[30] In general, the
FWHM value should follow a fixed value within a single layer,
and should be associated with the obvious slope change in the
phase transition region. However, we can find that in each con-
stituent layer, the Raman shift and the FWHM are not fixed
values or fluctuate along the horizontal line, but monotoni-
cally change. To be more intuitive, the variation trend is plotted
by different color arrows. The change rate of layer III is the
highest due to the obvious structural difference between II and
IV, which is in good agreement with the different XRD spectra
of layer III measured by micro-XRD. As a result, according to
the micro-XRD and line-scan Raman spectra collected along the
thickness of the multilayer composite, the formation of struc-
tural gradient is successfully verified.

The structural gradient and the interdiffusion between layers
will lead to the formation of strain gradient in the hierarchi-
cally structured KNN ceramics. In addition, the multilayer com-
posite can also synergistically combine the complementary
properties of each constituent layers (Figure S5, Supporting
Information). The multilayer capacitor configuration using
layers with different compositions has been proved to improve
the temperature stability of permittivity.[35] Of particular impor-
tance is that the concept of compositionally graded multilayer
composite can solve the key challenge of temperature instability
of d33 in KNN-based ceramics with PPB. Figure 3a shows the
temperature-dependent ε/εmax of the four single compositions.
Analogous to the Curie temperature (Figure S2, Supporting
Information), different TO-T values can be achieved by modi-
fying the BNZ content, accompanied by the broadening of the O-T phase transition peaks. Correspondingly, the piezoelectric
anomaly peaks are also located near their respective TO-T in the
four single compositions, as represented by the in situ temper-
ature dependence of d33 (Figure 3b). For KNN-based materials,
although the O-T PPB can greatly improve the piezoelectric
properties, it also brings serious temperature instability.[36] The
d 33 of the studied composition IV can reach up to 520 pC N−1
by shifting the TO-T toward room temperature (Table S1, Sup-
porting Information), but with a 45% degradation of the d33 up
to temperature of 100 °C (Figure 3b). As discussed in Figure 2
and Figure S2 in the Supporting Information, the phase transi-
tion gradient can be established by designing compositionally
graded multilayer composite, which is expected to greatly ben-
efit the temperature stability of the piezoelectricity. Figure 3c
shows the εr–T curves of the multilayer composite, where suc-
cessive and diffused O-T phase transitions can be achieved in
the temperature range of 25–112 °C (marked by the shadow),
due to which, a negligible piezoelectric variation below 6% can
be achieved. It is important to note that the composite struc-
ture plays an important role affecting the temperature stability
of the piezoelectricity, where the compositionally graded mul-
tilayer composite with I-II-III-IV and the same thickness of
each constituent layer exhibit the superior temperature stability
(Figure S6, Supporting Information). The new concept of the
phase transition gradient can effectively solve the temperature
instability of d33 inherently associated with PPB, which is not
achievable in conventional strategies including texture tech-
nique,[5,21,37] fine composition tuning,[26] and diffused phase
transition.[22]

The in situ d33–T of the structural gradient multilayer com-
posite is compared to the actively studied lead-free ceramic sys-
tems (Figure 3d),[17,38,39] demonstrating a superior piezoelectric
temperature stability in the temperature range of 25–100 °C.
Although ultrahigh piezoelectricity was reported in barium
titanate (BT)-based ceramics,[38] their low Curie temperatures
(TC < 120 °C) lead to complete depolarization of the ceramics
with temperature approaching 100 °C. Similar phenomenon
was also reported in sodium niobate (NN)-based ceramics due
to the relatively low TC.[39] Figure 3e further summarizes the
piezoelectric variation [Δd33 = |(d33T/d33RT) − 1| *100%] of some
representative KNN-based ceramics in the temperature range
of 25–100 °C. It can be observed that there is always a large
d 33 variation reported in traditionally designed KNN-based cer
amics,[16,17,19,21,23,24] while the studied composite KNN-based
ceramics exhibit an average room temperature d33 value of
≈330 pC N−1 with variation below 6% up to 100 °C. In addi-
tion, the temperature stability of the important figure of merit
for sensor application (d33 × g33) can also be greatly improved
(Figure S7, Supporting Information). Therefore, the newly
designed lead-free KNN-based compositional-graded multilayer
composite ceramics show great potential in piezoelectric appli-
cations where temperature stability is critical.

As one of the most important features of ferroelectric
ceramics, ferroelectric domains are closely related to the proper-
ties of the material. The domain structure of ferroelectric mate-
rials is very active to thermal excitation, leading to property vari-
ation under thermal treatment.[18] The temperature dependence
of primitive domain indicates that the domain orientation will
change with temperature, especially for micro- or nanosized
domains, leading to a transition from microsized domains to
macrosized domains (Figure S8, Supporting Information).
In view of the important role of domains in temperature sta-
bility,[40] the thermal evolution of the domain structure in each
constituent of the multilayer composite is given in Figure 3f
and Figure S9 in the Supporting Information, which was meas-
ured from the cross-section of the composite. For constituent
I, almost no back-switching of the oriented domains can be
observed because its higher TO-T (≈112 °C) results in more
stable domain behavior including both the oriented domain
(Figure 3f ) and the primitive domains (Figure S6, Supporting
Information). However, for the other three constituents (II,
III, and IV), obvious domain back-switching behavior can be
observed when the measured temperature is higher than their
corresponding TO-T. Therefore, when the four compositions
with different phase transition temperatures are integrated
into one multilayer composite, the oriented domain can be
well maintained when the measurement temperature is lower
than their TO-T of each constituent. As depicted in Figure 3f, the
PFM phase images of each constituent measured at selected
temperatures (slightly lower than their respective TO-T) are pre-
sented to illustrate the complementary domain behavior. Each
constituent of the composite ceramic can provide very stable
oriented domain over the temperature range of 25 to 100 °C,
being conducive to the enhanced temperature stability of the
piezoelectric properties.

To further understand this phenomenon, we used phase-
field simulation to show the difference in temperature stability
of d33 between compositionally graded multilayer composite and each single layer constituent. In the phase-field method,
the time-dependent Ginzburg–Landau (TDGL) equation is
introduced to describe the temporal evolution of the polariza-
tion field for the ferroelectric system.[41] The composition gra-
dient of the multilayer composite is reduced to a discrete com-
position gradient, with the composition interval of Δx = 0.1%.
This setting is based on the interdiffusion behavior between
layers confirmed by micro-XRD and line-scan Raman spectra in
Figure 2. Figure 4a shows the multivariate surface of composi-
tion, temperature, and d33, revealing the changing tread of sim-
ulated d33 values along the compositionally graded direction in
the multilayer composite at different temperatures. Figure 4b
compares temperature stability of d33 between multilayer com-
posite and each single composition. It is interesting to note that
unlike the sharp change of the piezoelectric coefficient of single
constituent, the multilayer composite simulated by the phase-
field exhibits stable piezoelectricity over temperature range of
25 to 100 °C, in good agreement with our experimental obser-
vation given in Figure 3. In addition, it should be mentioned
that during poling where the field is greater across the layers
with lower permittivity,[42] the electric field applied on the mul-
tilayer composite should be sufficient for polarization rotation
and domain switching of each constituent. Figure 4c gives the
simulation of the phase volume fraction as a function of tem-
perature in the compositionally graded multilayer composite.
The coexistence of O and T phases can be maintained in the
entire temperature range, ensuring both high piezoelectricity
and temperature stability.

In summary, the artificially designed compositionally graded
multilayer KNN-based ceramics have been successfully fab-
ricated by the tape casting method, satisfactorily solving the
inherent disadvantage of temperature instability in d33. Ben-
efiting from the successive O-T phase transitions, the strain
gradient, and the complementary effects of each constituent
layer, the multilayer composite exhibits significantly improved
temperature stability of d33 with variation below 6% in the tem-
perature range of 25–100 °C, meanwhile maintaining a yet high
d 33 of 330 pC N−1. We believe that the multilayer composite
ceramics with engineered phase transitions will provide a new
approach to obtain high-performance functional materials with
excellent temperature stability.

实验部分

Materials Synthesis:(1−x)(K0.48Na0.52)(Nb0.955Sb0.045)O3-
xBi0.5Na0.5ZrO3-0.2%Fe2O3 (x = 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04) ceramic powders
were prepared by the conventional solid-state method. All raw
materials, including K2CO3 (99%), Na2CO3 (99.8%), Nb2O5 (99.5%),
Sb2O3 (99.99%), Bi2O3 (99.999%), ZrO2 (99%), and Fe2O3 (98%), were
weighted according to the nominal composition and planetary milled
for 12 h with ethanol media. The rotation per minute (RPM) for the
planetary milling was set to 257. The mixed slurries were dried and
calcined at 850 °C for 6 h. The calcined powder was planetary milled
with ethanol for 12 h to obtain the matrix powder. For tape casting, the
slurries were prepared by mixing the matrix powders with ethanol and
dispersant (triethanolamine) using high energy ball milling for 2 h, and
then organic binder (polyvinyl alcohol aqueous solution) and plasticizer
(polyethylene glycol) were added into the slurries and ball milled for
another 3 h. The RPM during high energy ball milling was set to 600.
The homogenous slurries were tape casted on glass plate with a scraper height of 0.2 mm. After drying in a 50 °C oven for 30 min, these dried
tapes were cut, stacked, and laminated at 60 °C and 10 MPa for 15 min
to obtain green compacts, where a heated mold was used. After the
organic burnout at 850 °C for 2 h with a heating rate of 1 °C min−1,
the samples were sintered at 1085 °C for 3 h. In this work, the single
compositions of x = 0.01, 0.02, 0.03, 0.04 were abbreviated as I, II, III, IV.
And the compositionally graded multilayer composite was abbreviated
as I-II-III-IV, where the thickness of each constituent layer could be
tailored by the layer numbers of dried tapes. For example, the composite
(I-II-III-IV) with 10/10/10/10 means that the layer numbers of dried tapes
for constituent I, II, III, and IV were 10, 10, 10, and 10, respectively. To
obtain the compositionally graded multilayer composite, the dried tapes
of each constituent were selected and laminated in a sequential order.
For example, the composite (I-II-III-IV) represented 10 layer numbers of
dried tapes of constituent I, II, III, and IV stacking in sequence.

Structure Characterization: The crystal structure was determined
using XRD measurement with Cu Kα radiation (Rigaku, D/Max2500,
Tokyo, Japan). Micro-XRD was performed with a Rigaku Rapid IIR
(Japan), using a collimator of 100 µm to measure a narrow position of
the composite. Line-scan Raman spectroscopy was performed with a
Renishaw inVia Confocal micro-Raman spectrometer. For microstructure
characterization, field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM,
agellan400, FEI Company) was performed on the samples. The domain
morphology of the polished samples was characterized on a piezoelectric
force microscopy (PFM) by a commercial microscope (MFP-3D, Asylum
Research, Goleta, CA) applied to a conductive Pt−Ir-coated cantilever
EFM-10 (Nano World). The Litho-PFM mode was used in the domain
writing process, during which a voltage of −20 V was first applied to the
reorientation of the domains in an area of 2 × 2 µm, and then positive
voltage of 20 V was applied to a smaller region (1 × 1 µm) within the poled areas. After that, a small AC voltage of 2 V with frequency of 1 Hz
was applied to scan the domains at different temperatures.

Property Characterizations: The thickness of samples for electrical
measurement was 0.8–1 mm. Temperature dependence of dielectric
constant (εr–T curves) in the temperature range from room temperature
to 500 °C could be measured using a dielectric spectroscopy test
system (TZDM-200-300, Harbin Julang Technology Co. Ltd., Harbin
China), with a frequency of 1, 10, 100 kHz. To obtain more precise phase
transition information, εr–T curves of the ceramics were also conducted
by commercial dielectric spectrometer instrument (Novocontrol
Concept 80, Novocontrol, Germany). The piezoelectric coefficient
d33 was measured using a quasi-static piezoelectric constant testing
meter (ZJ-3A, Institute of Acoustics, Chinese Academy of Sciences).
In situ temperature dependence of d33 could be measured by a wide-
temperature-range d33 meter (TZFD-900, Harbin Julang Technology Co.
Ltd., Harbin, China). Field-dependent parameters including polarization
hysteresis loops (P–E), bipolar strain (S–E), and unipolar strain (S–E)
were measured with ferroelectric measuring equipment (aixACCT TF
Analyzer 2000, Germany), with a frequency of f = −2 Hz.
